## Measuring Skewness at a Glance

When a distribution is **skewed**, one side stretches farther than the other. That means the data is **not balanced evenly** around the center.

A common classroom shortcut is to compare the **mean** and the **median**:
- If **mean > median**, the distribution is usually **right-skewed**
- If **mean < median**, the distribution is usually **left-skewed**

This works because the **mean** is pulled more strongly by very large or very small values, while the **median** is more resistant to extreme values.

So:
- In a **right-skewed** distribution, the long right tail pulls the mean upward
- In a **left-skewed** distribution, the long left tail pulls the mean downward

That makes **mean vs. median** a useful way to spot the **direction** of skew quickly.

However, this method has limits. It does **not tell you exactly how much skew** there is, and it can sometimes be misleading. Two datasets might show a similar mean-median pattern while still having very different shapes.

For a more exact measurement, statisticians use a value called **skewness**. Skewness is the formal statistic that measures how asymmetric a distribution is.

So at a glance, you can use **mean vs. median** as a quick clue. But to understand skew more fully, people often look at all three of these together:
- **Mean vs. median**
- **Histogram or boxplot**
- **Skewness value**

In short, **mean and median help you guess the direction of skew**, while **skewness measures it more formally**.

In statistics, variance measures how spread out data is around the mean. Its possible values go from zero up to positive infinity, written as `[0, infinity)`.

The bracket **`[`** at **0** means **0 is included** in the range. That makes sense because variance can actually equal zero when all data points are exactly the same. The parenthesis **`)`** at **infinity** means infinity is **not included**. That is because variance can keep getting larger and larger without limit, but it never reaches a final value called infinity.

A variance of zero means there is no spread at all in the data. Every value is identical to the mean, so there is no deviation. As the variance gets larger, it means the data points are more spread out from the mean.

Variance is calculated by finding the difference between each data point and the mean, squaring each difference, and then averaging those squared values. Squaring makes all values positive and gives more weight to points that are farther from the mean. Because of this, variance is useful for measuring overall spread, though its units are squared rather than the original data units.
